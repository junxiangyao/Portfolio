Our Community Partner

The Children's Creativity Museum (CCM) in San Francisco is an interactive museum who offers hands-on, multimedia arts and technology experiences designed to build creative confidence in children ages 2-12. Their mission is to nurture creativity and collaboration in all children and families by providing opportunities for creative expression, innovation, and critical thinking.

Challenge

Children’s Creativity Museum wants to broaden their impact and create a more immersive educational experience.

As the importance of creativity is recognized as an essential 21st century skill in education, CCM is dedicated to help children build creative confidence by nurturing expression, innovation, and critical thinking. However, CCM faces limits in its ability to serve families who do not visit the facility and would like to build capacity and broaden its impact outside its walls.

How might we help propagate CCM’s mission and increase their impact beyond its physical space?

Solution

Children's Creativity App aims at spreading CCM's mission to develop children's creative confidence, even when they are not physically visiting the museum. Following CCM's philosophy, "Imagine, Create, Share," our solution provides open-ended activity prompts for children to cultivate their creativity. Parents can enjoy valuable parent-child time by creating together and documenting the process.

1.Activity prompts to encourage open-ended thinking.

Children's Creativity App has a collection of prompts for creative activities in different categories written by educators from CCM. The prompts are open-ended without forcing a specific outcome, allowing children to exercise their creative freedom and explore endless possibilities.

2. Documentation of creative work to reflect progress.

Parents could take photos and videos to document the works their children made. Our app allows parents to add stickers and notes to the pictures and videos. These artifacts will be saved in a personal gallery for children documenting their growth in creativity.

3. Community space to share tips, images, and appreciation.

CoffeeChat uses coffee cups as credits to pay for the service as this app aims to retain and encourage the coffee chat, which is a great way for students to chat with professionals before the pandemic. The amount of payment will be decided by how much they use this platform.

4. Merchandise store to celebrate creativity.

Children's creative works can be converted to cute and fun merchandise like photo books, phone cases, T-shirts, and greeting cards on the app's store. And this merchandise store provides a direct source of income.

Research

After secondary research, we spent days in the museum volunteering and immersively observing how they worked and interacted with their users. We then sent out surveys to understand the value of creative learning and the needs of parents.

  • We interviewed parents and educators from CCM to understand their thoughts on creative learning and their experience in the museum.

  • "I think every activity that my child does or is exposed to can help develop creativity. Having my child engage in experiences would enlarge his pool of thinking. The difficulty is to find those fun activities for him."


    -- Jeok, Parent

  • "Everyone (Children) of all ages is just so happy to see what they create come to life with the help of technology. They're proud of what they have created ... I get to see little kids from one-year-olds to even parents being so happy coloring in a house or car. It's a family activity."


    -- Fidelina, Museum Educator

Synthesis

Covid-19 hit San Francisco while we were in the research phase. As we cannot meet in person anymore, and the museum was closed due to the pandemic, we had to synthesize our data remotely and use alternative research methods like role-playing and co-designing to gain a deeper understanding of the problem with the help of Zoom and Miro.

Insights

  • Creative learning does not occur in isolation; it exists in interactions with the environment.

  • Parents associate their child's learning with tangible outcomes. However, creative learning is more process learning than product outcome.

  • CCM as an organization cannot rely only on an income-based only on its physical space (memberships, tickets, rental). They need to expand and branch out more digitally.

    • - DESIGN OPPORTUNITY -

    How might we take CCM's physical space and translate it into a digital product to spread creative confidence in children from home?

    Iteration

    Design Principles

  • Explorative

    Focus on process learning and not product creation.

  • Encouraging

    Building a supportive environment for creation and expression on the platform.

  • Energetic

    Playful, illuminative, and cheerful tone to spark creativity.

    • Prototyping

    We iterated 9 times on our solution. We also did 3 rounds of testing within 6 weeks. The goals of each round are:

    • Round 1 - Rapid prototyping and testing the desirability of our features.

    • Round 2 - Testing refined features and their flows.

    • Round 3 - Testing higher fidelity prototype with refined Flows.

    Usability Testing

    Most of the testing sessions were conducted through Zoom. We used Figma, and Google Slides to facilitate the testing sessions. We gathered our feedbacks into rainbow spreadsheets and refined our design accordingly.


    Design Decisions


    Activity Prompts Page

    Activities curated by the educators are displayed as cards. In our previous iterations, we had activities without images to allow the generation of open-ended ideas for children and save more screen real estate for other content.

    However, while testing, we learned that parents prefer seeing images to understand what this activity is about. So we added a cover image. We removed the check box for material gathering since people find it unnecessary.



    Activity Instructions

    One challenge is to balance between reducing the guidance to encourage active exploration and providing enough information in case children are stuck. Also, from testing, we realized that parents want to limit screen time for their children.

    Thus, we specifically chose not to put video content and other supportive information directly in the instructions so that children could focus more on creating by themselves. They could find those information on the hint page if needed.



    Using Claps instead of Likes

    For feedback metrics, users can clap multiple times for other children's work. This feature could help create a supportive environment that focuses on encouraging rather than competing.

    Compared with other binary feedback metrics (eg. Like), this aligns more with our first insight: creative learning occurs in the interactions with supportive environments.

    Metrics

    Here are 5 metrics that should be tracked to measure how well our solution helps CCM increase positve impact:

    1. The average number of pictures and videos taken during each activity.
    2. The percentage of people who share their pictures/clap on projects shared by others on the community page.
    3. The number of people that reached the final screen for purchasing merchandise by checking out.
    4. Income generated from the app through ticket and merchandise sales every month.
    5. Tracking the monthly active users.

    Future Steps

    Expanding to New Platforms
    As people mentioned they want a tablet version of this app for their kid to follow the activity prompts, it is a clear next step for us. Also, we will create a web app to better leverage CCM's powerful domain name, creativity.org.

    Integrating Onsite Activities
    To create a more holistic solution, we will look into opportunities to buld connection between our current solution with CCM's other onsite activities and exhibits.

    Creating channels for CCM educators to host activities live on the app.
    In our solution, the prompts and instructions would be written by CCM's educator. One potential direction is to push this a step further. As a supportive environment and appropriate guidance are essential for cultivating chidlren's creativity, we could creating channels for CCM educators to host activities live on the app.

    Reflections

    Keeping an open mind for change.
    When COVID-19 hit San Francisco in March, our design research is still in progress. There are a lot of unexpected changes in this process. Our major directions pivoted 4 times due to unforeseen constraints. I realized that keeping an open mind for change is critical from the experience of completing this project. Otherwise, we may miss the chance to leverage the advantages we still possess.

    Communication is the key.
    As we are a team of 5 designers, keeping everyone on the same page is necessary but difficult, not to mention facilitating remote collaborations within such an uncertain time. As the team's project manager, I learned that it is essential to find the minimum level of consensus to make decisions and move forward and to make sure our effort and content are not lost in our communication.
